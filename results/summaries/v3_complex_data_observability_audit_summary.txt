## Judgment

Small samples have clearly weaker Bz signals than large samples across train/val/test. The mean max_abs_bz rises from small to large, and the same trend holds for peak_to_peak_bz and l2_energy_bz.
The train / val / test small ratios are similar enough for the three splits to be considered broadly aligned; the test set is not unusually small-heavy.
Bz peak x is not a perfectly reliable proxy for defect centroid x. Small samples often have millimeter-level peak-centroid offsets, and multi-defect samples can further decouple the strongest Bz peak from the geometric centroid.
Low-signal samples are concentrated most strongly in polygon and rotated_rect, while multi_defect is less dominant in the low-signal bucket because multi-defect samples often have larger total area and stronger aggregate signal.
CURRENT_BASELINE failure samples correspond to weaker Bz signal and smaller true_area on average. pred_area=0 samples have much lower signal strength than nonzero predictions. IoU is moderately correlated with signal strength, while peak-centroid offset has only weak negative correlation with IoU/Dice.
Main conclusion: small-defect failure is partly a data observability / single-signal identifiability issue, especially for weak polygon and rotated_rect samples, but not purely a data impossibility. The moderate correlations and nonzero peak-centroid effects indicate that model training and representation still contribute to the failure.
